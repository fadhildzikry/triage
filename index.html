Module 01
Emergency Severity Index (ESI v4)
The globally validated 5-level triage system. Your core tool for daily ER flow. Risk stratification, not diagnosis — answer one question: how long can this patient safely wait?
⚠
Core Principle: Triage is a time-bounded clinical decision, not a diagnostic workup. If you are spending more than 2–3 minutes on an ESI-3/4/5 patient, you are diagnosing, not triaging.

Resource Count Guide for ESI-3/4/5

A "resource" = any diagnostic test, IV access/medication, or simple procedure. Physician assessment alone = 0 resources.

✓ COUNTS AS RESOURCE
  • ▸Labs (each panel = 1 resource)
  • ▸ECG
  • ▸IV access + IV fluids or IV medication
  • ▸X-ray (plain film)
  • ▸CT scan
  • ▸Ultrasound (POCUS or formal)
  • ▸Simple procedure (foley, NGT, splinting)
  • ▸Specialty consult resulting in procedure
✗ DOES NOT COUNT
  • ▸History taking
  • ▸Physical examination
  • ▸Prescription only (no IV/IM)
  • ▸Oral medication
  • ▸Simple wound care (clean + bandage)
  • ▸Telephone consult
  • ▸Saline lock without fluids/meds
🔑
The ESI-2 Mental Test: Ask yourself — "Would I be comfortable leaving this patient in the waiting room for the next 30–60 minutes?" If the answer is NO or even MAYBE — assign ESI-2. Err on the side of safety at this decision point.
Module 02
Routine High-Volume ER Triage
Systems, workflows, and cognitive frameworks for managing a packed ER without missing critical patients or creating bottlenecks.
The 2-Minute Rule — ESI 3/4/5

For stable patients, your triage contact should take ≤ 2 minutes. Sequence strictly:

  1. Chief complaint — one sentence, in the patient's words. Don't interpret yet.
  2. All 5 vitals — HR, BP, RR, SpO₂, Temp. Non-negotiable. Every patient. Always.
  3. Visual gestalt — "sick or not sick?" Trust this. Your pattern recognition is real data.
  4. Targeted question — one or two clarifying questions if vital signs or gestalt trigger concern.
  5. Resource prediction — mentally count: how many resources will this visit require?
  6. Pain score — if complaint is pain-related. Scale 0–10. Brief.
  7. Assign and document — ESI level, brief note, triage time stamped.
⛔
You are NOT taking a full history at triage. Resist this strongly. History-taking at triage blocks flow and delays care for subsequent patients.

High-Volume ER Flow Strategies
System-Level Interventions
  • ▸
    Triage-Initiated Protocols (TIPs): Standing orders for ECG (any cardiac complaint), troponin, CBC, plain X-ray, urine dipstick — ordered at triage before physician contact. Compresses door-to-data time by 30–45 min.
  • ▸
    Fast Track / Vertical Triage: ESI-4/5 routed to dedicated area. Must never share resus resources.
  • ▸
    Re-triage Protocol: Any ESI-3 patient waiting > 60 min gets reassessment — vitals + brief interval history.
  • ▸
    Waiting Room Surveillance: Visible clinical staff in or near waiting room. Passive observation catches clinical deterioration.
Cognitive Strategies for the Triage Nurse/Doctor
  • ▸
    Illness Script Matching: Build mental prototypes for every common ESI-2 presentation. Pattern recognition > stepwise reasoning under time pressure.
  • ▸
    Peripheral Vision: Continuously scan the waiting room, not just the patient in front of you. The deteriorating patient is rarely the loudest one.
  • ▸
    The "Worried Look" Sign: If a family member looks frightened, that's data — they know the patient's baseline, you don't.
  • ▸
    Rotate triage personnel: Error rates increase after 2 hours at triage desk. Rotate roles every 2 hours where staffing permits.
🇮🇩 Indonesia-Specific Context
  • ▸BPJS Redirection: ESI-4/5 patients on BPJS rujukan non-darurat pathway may be appropriately redirected to Puskesmas/Faskes Tingkat 1. Document clearly that triage was performed and the clinical basis for non-emergency classification.
  • ▸Tropical Fever — Lower Threshold: Any febrile patient with recent travel, outdoor exposure, or agricultural occupation → consider malaria, dengue, typhoid, leptospirosis in your ESI-2 threshold. Dengue with warning signs = ESI-2.
  • ▸Language: Triage documentation in Bahasa Indonesia. Ensure the intake nurse can conduct the assessment in the patient's primary language. Miscommunication at triage is a patient safety event.
  • ▸Motorcycle trauma: High-velocity mechanism even at moderate speeds. No helmet = automatic trauma screen regardless of chief complaint.
Module 03
START Triage Algorithm
Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment. The global standard for field/pre-hospital MCI triage. Target: <60 seconds per patient. You do NOT treat during primary START — you tag and move.
🚨
START Paradigm Shift: You are not trying to save one patient — you are trying to save the maximum number. During primary START triage, the only interventions allowed are airway repositioning and tourniquet for exsanguinating hemorrhage. Nothing else. If you stop to intubate, you are making an error in judgment, not heroism.
🔴 RED — Immediate
Life-threatening injury, survivable with immediate intervention.

Treat first. Transport first.
🟡 YELLOW — Delayed
Serious injury, physiologically stable. Can safely wait 30–60 min.

Treat after all REDs are stabilized.
🟢 GREEN — Minor
Walking wounded. Self-ambulatory. Minor injuries.

Treat last. Can self-direct to collection point.
⬛ BLACK — Expectant
Dead or unsurvivable injury given current resources.

Minimal intervention. Do not expend resources.

Permitted Interventions During START
  • ▸Manual airway opening — jaw thrust, chin lift, head tilt (if no cervical concern)

Module 04
SALT Triage Algorithm
Sort, Assess, Life-saving interventions, Treatment/Transport. More comprehensive than START, endorsed by CHEMM and FEMA. Allows immediate life-saving interventions (LSIs) during sorting — more realistic to actual field conditions.
ℹ
START vs SALT: SALT's key advantage is the explicit LSI step during initial sort — a patient who is BLACK by START due to apnea may be survivable with a 5-second jaw thrust. SALT formalizes this. Use SALT when trained personnel and minimal equipment are available at point of injury.
SALT — Step-by-Step
S
Step 1 — SORT (Global)
Globally sort the scene. Call out loudly: "Everyone who can walk, move to [designated area] now!"

This identifies GREEN (walking) patients immediately without individual assessment. Walking patients are lowest priority — direct them to a collection point.

Then call: "If you cannot walk but can wave or make noise, do so!" — these are your likely YELLOW/RED patients who need individual assessment next.

Patients who do not respond to either command → assess first (likely RED or BLACK).
A
Step 2 — ASSESS (Individual)
Prioritize assessment in order: (1) Still/unresponsive → (2) Waving/calling out → (3) Walking.

For each individual: Breathing? Pulse? Major hemorrhage? Mental status? This is your primary survey — ABCDE condensed to 30–60 seconds.
L
Step 3 — LIFE-SAVING INTERVENTIONS
Perform only if takes < 60 seconds and is life-saving. These LSIs are performed during assessment, not after:
  • Massive hemorrhage control — tourniquet application, wound packing
  • Airway maneuver — jaw thrust, recovery position, NPA if available
  • Needle decompression — tension pneumothorax (if clinically obvious + equipment available)
  • Autoinjector antidotes — in CBRN incidents (atropine for nerve agent)
  • Chest seals — open pneumothorax, sucking chest wounds
T
Step 4 — TAG (Classification)
After LSI, classify each patient:
🔴 IMMEDIATE
Life threat. Survivable. LSI performed or needed.
🟡 DELAYED
Serious, stable. Can wait. No LSI needed urgently.
🟢 MINIMAL
Minor injury. Walking wounded. Ambulatory.
⬛ EXPECTANT
Unsurvivable given resources. No LSI response. Not breathing after repositioning.
T
Step 5 — TREATMENT / TRANSPORT
Move patients to treatment area in priority order. Coordinate with transport officer for hospital destination matching by injury type and receiving capacity. Secondary triage occurs at the treatment area.

Scene Setup — First 5 Minutes
🚨
First responder priority: Scene safety before patient contact. An incapacitated responder becomes a casualty. Identify hazards: structural instability, fire, chemical/gas, traffic, secondary blast threat.
  1. Size up the scene: Estimate casualty number, mechanism, ongoing hazard, resource needs. Transmit to incident command within 60 seconds.
  2. Establish upwind/uphill/upstream CCP: Casualty Collection Point must be outside the hazard zone. Direct all ambulatory patients here immediately.
  3. One-way patient flow: Triage → Treatment → Transport. Patients move forward only. Never backward through the system. Mark with colored flags or cones if available.
  4. Designate triage officer: This person does nothing except triage. No treatment. No administration. No phone calls. Triage only.
  5. Establish communications: Dedicated radio channel for medical branch. Transport officer maintains hospital capacity log.
  6. Log every patient: Triage tag = patient record. Write time tagged, interventions performed, tag color, destination. Medico-legal and epidemiological importance.
📍
CCP Placement Rule: Upwind (avoid smoke/chemical), uphill (avoid flooding/liquid hazard), upstream (avoid contaminated water flow). If in an earthquake — away from buildings. In Indonesia: consider volcanic ash, tsunami secondary wave risk, unstable ground near epicenter.
GREEN Patients as a Resource
💡
Walking wounded are not a burden — they are your most available resource. Redirect ambulatory GREEN patients to assist with: directing other victims, applying manual pressure, holding IVs, basic comfort measures, relaying communications. This dramatically reduces your effective staff-to-patient ratio.
The Black Tag — Expectant Decision
⚠
This is one of the most psychologically difficult clinical decisions in medicine. Black-tagging a breathing patient is correct when their injuries are incompatible with survival given available resources. It is not abandonment — it is reallocation of limited resources to maximize lives saved.
Examples of Expectant (Field Conditions)
  • ▸Bilateral open pneumothorax with fixed/dilated pupils
  • ▸Massive burns >80% BSA in resource-limited MCI
  • ▸Traumatic brain injury with GCS 3 + fixed pupils + no respiratory effort after airway opening
  • ▸Penetrating trauma to head with obvious brain herniation
  • ▸High cervical spinal cord injury with apnea — when ventilators are unavailable
Re-Triage Principle

Expectant status is not permanent. If resources free up — e.g., RED patients are transported and the situation de-escalates — EXPECTANT patients are reassessed and may be upgraded.

🔄
Secondary triage at the treatment area is more thorough. Expectant tags assigned at field may be revised when a physician does secondary assessment with more information.
Module 06
Hospital-Side MCI Response
When the disaster comes to your door. Managing unannounced surges, activating internal disaster plans, and maintaining ER function during mass casualty intake.
🚨
Critical Rule: Activate your Hospital Disaster Plan the moment you become aware of an MCI — do NOT wait for official declaration. Every minute of delay in activation = compromised surge capacity. In Indonesia, declare Kode Merah Bencana internally as your first act.
Immediate Actions Upon MCI Notification — First 10 Minutes
  1. Declare Hospital Disaster Code — activate on-call cascade immediately. Surgery, Anaesthesia, Orthopedics, Neurosurgery, Blood Bank, ICU, Security, Administration.
  2. Decongest the ER: Identify all admittable patients → fast-track to wards. Discharge stable observation patients. Move ESI-3 from resus bays to hallway spaces. Every resus bay cleared = one RED patient saved.
  3. Designate Entrance Triage Officer: One senior clinician at ER entrance doing nothing but secondary triage. This person does not move inside the ER.
  4. Establish color-coded zones: Physical demarcation of RED/YELLOW/GREEN treatment areas. Assign a team leader per zone, not per patient.
  5. Activate Family Holding Area: Designate a separate space away from clinical areas for family members. Assign a family liaison. This prevents secondary chaos that compounds clinical load.
  6. Blood bank emergency protocol: Pre-position O-negative uncrossmatched blood for RED area. Activate massive transfusion protocol.
  7. Document intake centrally: One admin staff maintaining MCI patient log at ER entrance. Name/ID, tag color, time of arrival, destination bed.
  8. Communicate with scene: Establish radio/phone contact with field triage officer for patient count, injury types, and ETA.
Secondary Triage at Hospital Arrival

All field-tagged patients get secondary triage on hospital arrival. Field conditions and hospital conditions are different. Time elapsed since field triage = clinical change.

Crowd and Family Management
⚠
Underappreciated MCI threat: Family members flooding the ER is one of the most disruptive factors in hospital MCI response. A crowd of 200 panicked family members can functionally shut down clinical operations. This must be anticipated and managed proactively.
  • ▸Pre-designated family zone: Separate from all clinical areas. Cafeteria, auditorium, or outdoor space. Staffed by social worker or chaplain.
  • ▸Patient tracking board: Public-facing (de-identified) status board updated every 30 minutes. Reduces individual family queries by 70%.
  • ▸Security at clinical boundary: One-way access. No family in treatment areas during active MCI phase.
  • ▸Family liaison officer: Dedicated clinician or senior nurse. Single point of communication. Updates families in batches, not individually.
  • ▸Media perimeter: Media must never access clinical or family areas. Designate media liaison point at hospital entrance.
🇮🇩 Indonesian Hospital MCI Context
  • ▸BPBD Coordination: Engage Badan Penanggulangan Bencana Daerah (provincial/district) early — they control resource allocation and legal authority during declared disasters. Your hospital disaster plan must include the BPBD contact protocol.
  • ▸Dinkes chain of command: Dinas Kesehatan must be notified — they coordinate inter-hospital patient distribution and activate Rumah Sakit Lapangan (field hospitals) if needed.
  • ▸Communication failure: Cell towers overload during mass disasters in Indonesia. Ensure your hospital has HT (handy-talky) radio communication as primary backup. WIFI is unreliable. Satphone for hospital leadership.
  • ▸Blood supply: Contact PMI (Palang Merah Indonesia) immediately upon MCI declaration — they have emergency mobilization protocols for whole blood and components.
  • ▸Earthquake + crush injury: Indonesia is high-seismic-risk. Prepare for crush injury surge: hyperkalemia, rhabdomyolysis, acute kidney injury, compartment syndrome. Pre-position dialysis coordination contacts.

Module 08
Pits & Pearls — Master Reference
The consolidated cognitive errors and clinical insights that separate competent triage from expert triage. Know these. Drill these. They save lives.
⛔ Pit — The Quiet Critical
The sickest patient is rarely the loudest. The screaming patient with an ankle fracture draws attention; the quiet elderly patient in septic shock sits unnoticed. Actively scan for stillness, pallor, and audible labored breathing in the waiting room.
💡 Pearl — Vital Signs Before Gestalt
Patient appears well → but HR is 130 and RR is 26. Trust the numbers. "Looks okay" is a cognitive trap. Vital signs catch what pattern recognition misses. Measure all 5, every patient, before you form an impression.
⛔ Pit — Anchoring on First Complaint
Patient says "I fell down" and you think: fall → orthopedic. But why did they fall? Near-syncope, AF, hypoglycemia, TIA, acute MI. The presenting complaint is the door, not the room. One sentence of context prevents this.
💡 Pearl — The Worried Family Sign
Family members know the patient's baseline. You don't. If a family member looks frightened or says "he's never been like this" — treat that as a clinical finding. Upgrade. Investigate. Their fear is data.
⛔ Pit — ESI-2 Under-Triage
The most dangerous systematic error in ER triage. Sepsis presenting as "fever and fatigue," NSTEMI as "heartburn," SAH as "just a headache." The miss rate for ESI-2 diagnoses given ESI-3 triage is unacceptably high in high-volume ERs.
💡 Pearl — The 30-Minute Rule
If a patient has been waiting 30 minutes and their condition could have changed, retriage them. ESI-3 patients waiting longer than 60 minutes must have vitals retaken. A stable ESI-3 can become ESI-2 while waiting.
⛔ Pit — History at Triage
Taking a full history at triage is the most common cause of triage bottlenecks. Every minute spent on detailed history for one patient delays the vital sign assessment of the next patient — who may be ESI-2. Brief, targeted, limited to what changes the ESI level.
💡 Pearl — Triage-Initiated Protocols
Standing orders for ECG, troponin, CBC, urinalysis initiated at triage (before physician contact) compress door-to-data time by 30–45 minutes in chest pain, fever, and abdominal pain presentations. This is evidence-based workflow, not overstepping.
⛔ Pit — Treating During Primary Triage
The most lethal MCI triage error. Stopping to intubate one patient for 3 minutes while 10 others are unassessed is worse than leaving one patient unintubated. Primary triage moves. Always. Treatment is for the treatment phase.
💡 Pearl — Tourniquet Early and Decisively
TCCC data from Iraq/Afghanistan conflicts is unambiguous: early tourniquet application (before shock) saves lives. Late application (in shock) is frequently ineffective. In MCI, tourniquet hesitation is malpractice by omission.
⛔ Pit — Tunnel Vision on Dramatic Injury
The patient with an open femur fracture and blood everywhere is visually compelling. The patient 3 meters away with a developing tension pneumothorax is quiet. Systematic sorting prevents fixation on the most visually striking casualty.
💡 Pearl — Green Patients as First Responders
Ambulatory GREEN patients can apply manual pressure, hold IV bags, direct other victims, and relay information. Deploying them multiplies your effective manpower immediately. Brief instruction: "Go to that tent. Tell them to lie down. Hold this on the wound."
⛔ Pit — Emotional Override on Pediatric Casualties
Children are triaged by the same physiological rules as adults (use JumpSTART modification). Emotional response to a child's appearance or distress must not override the algorithm. Black-tagging an unsurvivable pediatric casualty is objectively correct in true MCI.
💡 Pearl — The Walk Test is Physiological
Asking "can you walk?" does more than sort ambulatory patients — it simultaneously screens for catastrophic neurological, orthopedic, and hemodynamic compromise. A patient who is cardiovascularly shocked cannot walk. The walk test is a rapid physiological screen.
⛔ Pit — Failure to Re-Triage
A YELLOW patient who received a tourniquet at scene and is now actively deteriorating in the treatment area must be identified. Secondary triage at the treatment area is a clinical skill, not a formality. Assign dedicated staff to reassess tagged patients every 10–15 min.
💡 Pearl — Expectant Tag is Revisable
As RED patients are transported and resources free up, EXPECTANT patients are reassessed and upgraded if appropriate. Expectant means unsurvivable given current resources — not permanently untreatable. Resource flux = triage revision.
⛔ Pit — Waiting for Official Declaration
The biggest hospital-side MCI error. Waiting for official disaster declaration before activating surge protocols wastes 15–30 minutes of critical preparation time. Activate internally the moment you have credible information of a mass casualty event.
💡 Pearl — Decongest First, Treat Second
The single highest-yield hospital MCI action is rapid ER decongestion: discharge all ESI-4/5, fast-track all admittable patients to wards, move stable observation cases to hallways. Every resus bay created = one RED patient's life potentially saved.
⛔ Pit — Per-Patient Team Assignment
In routine ER, each patient has a team. In MCI, this model fails immediately. Assign teams to ZONES, not patients. One team per zone provides consistent coverage and prevents the abandonment of newly arriving patients while staff are occupied with existing ones.
💡 Pearl — Family Liaison Saves Clinical Capacity
One dedicated clinician or senior nurse assigned exclusively to family communication eliminates hundreds of individual family queries to clinical staff. A single family liaison with a status board update every 30 min reduces clinical interruption by >70%.
⛔ Pit — Neglecting Field Communication
Not knowing how many patients are en route, their injury profile, or their ETA means you cannot position resources. Establish radio communication with field triage officer within 5 minutes of activation. Transport officer role is critical here.
💡 Pearl — Re-Triage Every Incoming Patient
Field-assigned tags reflect conditions at the scene minutes ago. A RED patient may have stabilized en route; a YELLOW patient may have deteriorated. Every MCI patient gets secondary triage at the ER entrance. The entrance triage officer has the authority to revise any field tag.
The 7 Cognitive Errors in Triage

Understanding your own cognitive vulnerabilities is a clinical skill. These errors occur in both routine and disaster triage. Name them to interrupt them.

ErrorMechanismTriage ExampleCountermeasure
Anchoring Over-weighting first impression "Chest pain? He's 25 and fit — must be musculoskeletal." Always complete vital signs before assigning ESI. Vitals-first rule.
Premature closure Stopping reasoning once a likely diagnosis is found "It's a UTI — explained the abdominal pain." (misses ectopic) One-question habit: "What else could this be?"
Availability bias Over-estimating diagnoses recently seen After a dengue case: triaging every fever as probable dengue Explicit vital sign thresholds for upgrade — objective anchors
Framing effect Accepting the presenting narrative as complete truth "She says it's just anxiety" — misses STEMI in young woman Treat every complaint as potentially lethal until vitals confirm otherwise
Visceral bias Liking or disliking a patient affects clinical judgment Familiar frequent-flier patient under-triaged despite new serious complaint Acknowledge the bias. Apply the ESI algorithm uniformly. Document.
Commission bias Urge to do something leading to over-assessment Full history-taking at triage, delaying queue by 5+ min per patient 2-minute rule. Vital signs first. History later, by treating team.
Fatigue-induced bias Cognitive decline after prolonged duty ESI error rates rise after 2 hours at triage desk Mandatory triage rotation every 2 hours. Non-negotiable policy.
